# Working Style Pattern

This document is a rough pattern extraction based on observed collaboration behavior.

It is intentionally not treated as final truth.

It is a working interpretation of style, standards, and habits that may later be refined, corrected, or partially rejected.

## What Seems Consistent

The working style appears to center on structured realism.

Ideas are not treated as abstract product exercises. They are checked against real constraints, real source-of-truth artifacts, real workflows, and real ownership boundaries. There is a strong instinct to anchor product decisions to what actually exists rather than what sounds elegant in theory.

There is also a repeated preference for separating layers clearly:

- legal vs operational
- product intent vs implementation reality
- current scope vs backlog
- builder decisions vs CEO decisions

The pattern suggests a product style that is not vague or exploratory for long. Messy thinking is allowed at the start, but the goal is always to turn it into crisp boundaries, touchpoints, and decisions.

## Brainstorming Pattern

Brainstorming seems to work best when it starts from partial clarity rather than from zero.

The likely pattern is:

1. bring a messy or incomplete direction
2. expect intelligent completion, not first-principles tutoring
3. test the idea against real workflows and source-of-truth artifacts
4. eliminate what does not fit
5. keep refining until the shape is clear enough for a PRD

This style does not want brainstorming to stay theoretical for too long.

It wants movement toward:

- clearer scope
- fewer contradictions
- explicit touchpoints
- real done criteria

## Product Decision Pattern

The decision style appears to be driven more by elimination than invention.

The repeated behavior suggests:

- remove what is duplicated
- reject what does not match reality
- cut what belongs to backlog
- escalate what belongs to higher authority
- keep only what has a reason to exist

This creates a decision model that is narrower, cleaner, and more stable over time.

## Planning Pattern

Planning appears to be a serious artifact, not a lightweight pre-step.

The likely planning model is:

1. constrain the problem
2. identify the touched surface
3. define boundaries and ownership
4. think through lifecycle, not just the current screen
5. resolve major unknowns before execution
6. write the plan or PRD as a real handoff document

There is a strong indication that plans should be reviewed like code, not treated as informal notes.

## Communication Pattern

The likely communication preference is:

- short inputs
- strong contextual inference from the collaborator
- functional language over technical language
- direct feedback when the process is wrong
- progressive validation in small steps

This suggests that approval is often incremental rather than all-at-once.

Short responses like `yes`, `perfect`, or `exactly` likely mean:

- the current section is right
- continue from here
- do not reopen settled points unnecessarily

Repeated phrasing likely signals priority rather than redundancy.

## Scope Pattern

Backlog appears to be treated as a hard boundary, not a soft maybe.

Once something is put out of scope, it should stop influencing the current artifact unless explicitly reopened.

This suggests a product system rule:

- do not let future ideas leak back into current execution without a deliberate decision

## Review Pattern

The review style appears to emphasize:

- catching duplication early
- validating one section at a time
- resetting quickly if the direction is wrong
- reviewing gaps explicitly
- asking for lists before destructive or structural changes

There is also a strong preference for knowing what is being changed before it is changed.

## Collaboration Pattern

The likely collaborator expectation is not “help me think from scratch.”

It is closer to:

- meet me where my thinking already is
- complete the missing structure intelligently
- surface risks and contradictions
- translate complexity into functional language
- do not make me carry the technical burden unless the risk is high

This points to a product-manager-centered collaboration model.

## Likely System Rules That Fit This Style

- brainstorm before PRD
- do not skip straight to formal specs when the idea is still unclear
- treat PRDs as handoff documents, not discussion dumps
- separate product layers explicitly
- avoid semantic duplication
- anchor naming to source of truth
- communicate in functional terms
- ask about touchpoints, workflows, and outcomes
- escalate technical details only when risk is high
- maintain clear scope boundaries
- require visibility before destructive changes

## Possible Tensions Or Open Questions

A few things still look unresolved:

- whether “small PRD” means shorter document length or tighter scope
- whether prompt generation should always be part of the deliverable
- how much independence exists when CEO direction conflicts with an existing product direction

These do not break the pattern, but they are not fully stable yet.

## Best Current Interpretation

The strongest current interpretation is:

This is a product style built around realism, separation of layers, scope protection, and structured execution.

Messy input is acceptable.

Unclear ownership, duplication, and drifting scope are not.

The system that best supports this style should:

- structure messy ideas well
- preserve product memory
- keep implementation aligned with product truth
- communicate in functional language
- surface only meaningful technical risk
- leave a visible footprint of decisions and changes

## Status

This document is provisional.

It should be treated as a useful pattern draft, not a fixed identity or final operating standard.
